## Exercises

> Hit peak FLOP
>
> Open your `peakflop.cpp` again and generalize the type used in the `peak` 
function. Then use `SIMD_BENCHMARK_TEMPLATE(peak, all_simds_of<float, 
IncludeBuiltin>);` instead of `BENCHMARK(peak)` to instantiate the template for 
different types.
{:.block-task}

> TIP
>
> How do you calculate FLOP?
>
> There's a useful trait in `"benchmark.h"`: `element_count<T>::value` will tell 
you how many scalar elements are stored in `T`. In contrast to `T::size()`, 
`element_count<T>::value` also works for non-`simd` types.
>
> Don't forget that we need to build with `-march=native`!
{:.block-tip}

> Benchmark linear search
>
> Now benchmark the linear search functions we implemented. Compare e.g. against 
`std::ranges::count`. [Boilerplate](#linear-search-benchmark-boilerplate)
{:.block-task}

> What limits performance?
>
> What is limiting performance? Estimate the theoretical performance limit. How 
far away is your result? Can you optimize loads and epilogue
>
> My implementation, executing on a Skylake Xeon, counts spaces in 11.7% of the 
time that `std::ranges::count` requires. That's 48.9 GB/s instead of 5.7 GB/s.
{:.block-warning}

> TIP
>
> `string_view::find_first_of` calls `memchr` in glibc, which is an ifunc 
resolving at runtime to a vectorization best suited to the current CPU. So don't 
expect to see useful speed-up. But can you still beat it?
{:.block-tip}
